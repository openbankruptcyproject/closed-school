Closed School Discharge

An administrative federal student loan discharge for borrowers whose schools closed during or shortly after enrollment. No bankruptcy filing required. Educational reference, not legal advice.

What this discharge is

The Closed School Discharge program eliminates federal student loan debt for borrowers whose schools closed before they could complete their program. The discharge is administered by the U.S. Department of Education and does not require a bankruptcy filing.

Closed School Discharge applies to Direct Loans, FFEL Program loans, and Perkins Loans used to attend the closed school. It does not apply to private student loans.

Eligibility

General eligibility framework

  • The school closed while the borrower was enrolled, or shortly after they withdrew (within a window set by Department of Education rules — the window has varied between 120 days and 180 days under different administrations and may be longer for certain school types).
  • The borrower did not complete the program of study at the closed school.
  • The borrower did not transfer the credits to a comparable program at another institution and complete that program (a “teach-out” would generally disqualify the borrower from this discharge).

Some closed-school groups have been the subject of group discharge orders by the Department of Education, in which all borrowers from a particular closed institution receive automatic discharge consideration without individual application.

Application process

Submit a Closed School Discharge application through the borrower's federal loan servicer. The application requires identifying the closed school, the dates of attendance, and confirmation that the program was not completed and credits were not transferred to a comparable program.

If approved, the loan balance is discharged and any payments previously made on that loan are typically refunded.

How it interacts with bankruptcy

Closed School Discharge eliminates the loan obligation entirely. A borrower who qualifies for Closed School Discharge generally does not need a § 523(a)(8) AP for the same loans. The two paths are not mutually exclusive in theory — pursuing one does not bar the other — but practically, borrowers usually pursue the administrative path first because it is simpler and faster.

Major closed-school cohorts with group discharges

The Department of Education has issued group discharges to borrowers from several large school closures, eliminating the need for individual application:

  • Corinthian Colleges (closed 2015) — Heald College, Everest, WyoTech. Group discharge orders covered most enrolled borrowers from the closure period.
  • ITT Technical Institute (closed 2016) — group discharge orders for borrowers enrolled at closure or who withdrew within the qualifying window.
  • Westwood College (closed 2015 with related findings).
  • Education Corporation of America / Brightwood / Virginia College (closed 2018).
  • Argosy University / Art Institutes (closed 2019).

If you attended one of these institutions, your loans may have already been discharged without you needing to apply. Check your servicer records and studentaid.gov.

The closed-school window

Standard eligibility requires that the school closed while the borrower was enrolled, or that the borrower withdrew within a window before closure. The window has varied:

  • 120 days for most loans through 2017
  • 180 days in updated rules
  • 1 year for some closures involving misconduct findings or where the Department determined exceptional circumstances

The exact window applicable to your closure depends on the dates and any applicable group discharge order. Check the Department of Education Closed School Discharge page for current rules.

What disqualifies you

Closed School Discharge does not apply if:

  • You completed your program of study at the closed school.
  • You transferred your credits to a comparable program at another institution and completed that program (a teach-out arrangement).
  • You withdrew well before the closure outside the qualifying window.
Important. This page describes the program in general terms. Eligibility determinations are fact-specific. Talk to a licensed bankruptcy attorney, a legal aid attorney, or a Department of Education ombudsman about your specific situation.
